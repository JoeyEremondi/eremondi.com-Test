I am not like you

Sep 8, 2019 3 min read

The writing of this post, in part, was triggered by me going through the US student visa process again and being more aware of how I feel about it. Points raised here are personal observations and may not apply to other people.

Students like me blend into the background of most schools in the US. We have slightly funny accents and a lack of pop culture knowledge but by and large can pass off as a normal student1. Behind the facade of normality, there is always a small voice in my head that keeps saying, “You’re not normal. You’re not like them. You don’t belong.” The triggers for this are small but constant: Sometime I mispronounce a word or don’t understand a common pop reference or plain old misunderstand what someone is saying. Other reminders are all the legal documents I own which say, “Temporary visitor”, “Non-immigrant Alien”, “Non-citizen”. Small, but constant reminders that I don’t belong.

International students like me shouldn’t have much to complain to: We get to study at amazing schools, doing cool and interesting research surrounded by people who care about us and accept us. For me, and I suspect for many others, these things come at the incredible cost of uprooting our former lives and moving far away from our families and people we care about. Along with this also comes the constant fear that a small misstep could cost us our lives. Anything from a small mistake on your taxes to an inconspicuous mistake on a visa application.

Next comes jobs. Working as an international student isn’t as simple as getting a job offer from an employer and showing up to work. Depending on the kind of job, internship or full-time employment, there is a whole host of work compliance related things need to be completed. For an internship, students have to apply for a Curricular Practical Training (CPT) which can be as simple as getting a letter from your department to enrolling and paying for a class at your school while you are employed. Optional Practical Training (OPT) comes into play when you graduate and want to work in the US. Students are required to acquire a job letter and apply for a work permit that can take up to three months to show up.

Attending conferences is just as annoying. While your friends can catch direct flights to most countries and not worry much about anything else, most international students have to get a visa from the country where the conference is located. Depending on the country and the student’s nationality, the process requires a lot of documentation with no guarantee that you’ll get a visa. If you work on a sensitive research topic like security or machine learning, getting a visa is equivalent to getting a winning lottery ticket.

There are hundreds of such items, all of which are reasonable in isolation but take together are a continuous reminder of how I am different. The somewhat tragic part of this blog post is that I genuinely believe that my experience is still better than most international students. I go an internationally recognizable school and work with people who are mostly aware and sympathetic to these issue.

I worry what happens to people not like me.
